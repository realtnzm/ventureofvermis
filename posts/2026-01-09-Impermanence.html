Terrified of being known, terrified of being unknown.

Sometimes, I let myself be known too well. I spill the details of my life as if they are harmless facts ---stories, memories, wounds dressed up as anecdotes. I speak freely, almost eagerly, believing that being understood is the same as being safe. Yet midway through my confession, I stop. The words dry up, my tongue turns heavy, and silence rushes in. It is not because I have nothing more to say, but because I suddenly remember who I'm conversing with: a phantom, a temporary presence, someone who may soon fade into irrelevance in my life.

This fear is a quiet contradiction I live with daily. I am terrified of being unknown --of drifting through relationships unseen, unheard, reduced to a surface-level existence. To be unknown feels like erasure, like screaming into a void where no echo returns. If no one truly knows me, do I fully exist? This fear pushes me to open up, to offer pieces of myself too quickly, to make myself familiar in hopes of becoming unforgettable.

And yet, I am equally terrified of being known.

To be known is to be exposed. It means allowing someone access to my softest truths, the parts of me that are unfinished, unpolished, and uncertain. Once spoken, these truths no longer belong solely to me. They live in someone else’s memory, subject to misinterpretation. What frightens me most is not vulnerability itself, but the possibility that my vulnerability will outlast the relationship it was offered to.

There is something haunting about giving someone the map to your inner world when they may soon leave the territory altogether. They become ghosts who know too much --phantoms carrying fragments of your story with no obligation to cherish them. The idea that someone unimportant could hold something so intimate feels like a quiet betrayal of the self. So I stop speaking. I retreat. I swallow my words whole, choosing mystery over regret.

This tension traps me in an emotional limbo. I oscillate between oversharing and silence, between craving connection and fearing its consequences. I want to be deeply seen, but only by those who will stay. I want to be remembered, but not by those who will forget me carelessly. I want intimacy with permanence, understanding without expiration.

Perhaps this fear reveals a deeper truth: I do not just fear being known or unknown—I fear impermanence. I fear that relationships, like moments, are fleeting, while the parts of myself I give away feel permanent. Until I learn how to sit with that impermanence, I will continue to pause mid-sentence, weighing every word, asking myself whether this listener has earned the right to know me.

In the end, I exist between two fears, both equally loud. To be unknown is lonely. To be known is dangerous. And somewhere between silence and confession, I am still learning how to protect myself without disappearing.
